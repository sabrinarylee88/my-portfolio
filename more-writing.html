I started to take writing more seriously when I noticed my thoughts and feelings about the world start to connect to each other in ways that they hadn't before.

It was around the time when I started getting involved with my university's writing center as a tutor.

Part of the work was studying the act of writing itself, and its relationship to thinking, language, memory, perception, culture.

I discovered aspects and benefits to writing that I had never considered before.

At the same time, I was early on in my time in New York, and it quickly occurred to me that no one was going to tell me how to be in this place where so many competing ideas about how to live existed.

During this time, writing helped me learn about people so different from me, while pushing me to getting clearer on who I wanted to be.

To me, the most brutal yet fascinating part of the writing process, is its unforgiving tendency to reveal truths that simply thinking or feeling often shies away from.

What I mean is, it's difficult to write something that the writer knows, in their body, isn't the truth.

Nowadays, I think about what's lost when writing is outsourced — when the body, and the friction, are removed from the process.
