I've been meaning to have a place for my work for a while now.

The thought of having to announce my existence like portfolios often do made me uneasy.

Plus, the task of building a space that could collapse my past, present and kind of future that I imagine for myself, was daunting.

While designing this space, I thought it should at least have these characteristics:

That would age well,

that felt like home, and that I would be excited to return to

and that was a piece of work on its own

I was born and raised in Singapore and so I'm used to tropical heat. My childhood smelt of rain mixed with soil and chlorine.

Growing up, we'd often visited my Dad's family in Sydney, Australia, which smelt like dry bark and old pillowcases, and my mom's in Shanghai, where the smell of fried meat and cigarette smoke.

I was also lucky enough to travel when I was young, mostly to neighboring countries around South East Asia. I would buy disposable cameras for our trips; I remember being in total awe after seeing my first roll of film of our trip to Vietnam.

The colors came out dripping with saturation, vinyl reds and tarpaulin greens, with the camera's flash bleaching metal tuk tuks and temple moldings to chrome.

I moved to New York for school kind of on a whim during the pandemic.

I remember sobbing on a stoop during move-in week because I couldn't find toilet paper at any Target in the city.

It was a typical New York welcome: no easing in, just starting.

The city's colors and sounds and people jangled my nerves.

I wasn't used to life being so big.

I lived with my three roommates in a walk-up apartment on Macdougal Street.

We filled it with a mess of cheap clothes, expired sauces and spices from home and too much estrogen for such a small space.

As time went on, I learned how to harness its energy without letting it drain me, and slowly, what used to scare me started to excite me.

I understood "becoming a New Yorker" as reaching a point where all other cities started to feel a little dim.

We graduated in 2024, on a rainy day in May. We ran in the rain, drank on the subway and danced in the streets.

I stayed on another messy year in New York, working while figuring out what the next step would look like.

But sometime around early 2025, I realized that I'd been so caught up in the motion, so close to the eye of the storm, that by the time I took a step back, the plot had already passed me by.

I left the city the summer of 2025 and spent the next year settling back into home.

I woke up to the cuckoo sounds of native birds in my childhood bedroom like it was all just a dream.

I spent the rest of the year doing the slow travel that I always dreamed of, listening to others stories while trying to make sense of my own.

In 2026, I came to my mother's hometown, Shanghai, to brush up on my Mandarin, something else I've always wanted to do.

What I didn't anticipate was that this task required its own emotional adjustment.

Not towards reaching or grasping, but learning to stop holding back the expressions that I'd always held in my body from, run free.

These days, between looking at complicated Chinese characters and Chinese apps that move too quickly, I find the time to watch elderly couples square dance in the park.

If you made it this far, thank you for being here.
