I was born and raised in Singapore and so I'm used to tropical heat. My childhood smelt of rain mixed with soil and chlorine.

Growing up, we'd often visited my Dad's family in Sydney, which smelt like dry bark and old pillowcases, and my mom's in Shanghai, where the smell of fried meat and cigarette smoke.

I was also lucky to travel when I was young, mostly to neighboring countries around South East Asia. I would buy disposable cameras for our trips; I remember the feeling of seeing the results of my first roll of film, our trip to Vietnam.

The colors came out dripping with saturation, vinyl reds and tarpaulin greens, with the camera's flash turning metal tuk tuks and temple moldings a stunning chrome.

I moved to New York for school kind of on a whim during the pandemic.

I remember crying on the street in my first week because I couldn't find toilet paper at any Target in the city.

It was a typical New York welcome: no easing in, just go.

The city's colors and sounds and people rubbed against my nerves.

I wasn't used to life being so big.

I lived with my three roommates in a walk-up apartment on Macdougal Street.

We filled it with a mess of cheap clothes, expired sauces and spices from home and too much estrogen for such a small space.

As time went on, I learned how to move with its energy without letting it drain me, and slowly, what used to scare me started to excite me.

We graduated in 2024, on a cloudy day in May. Just as we got into the line outside Yankees stadium, it started pouring rain.

I stayed another messy year in New York, working while figuring out what my next step would be.

But sometime around early 2025, I realized that I'd been so caught up in the motion, that by the time I turned around, the plot had already passed me by.

I left New York in the summer of 2025 and spent the next year settling back into home.

The cuckoo sounds of native Singapore birds seeped into my childhood bedroom in these early mornings, as if to tell me that that was all a dream.

I spent the rest of the year doing the slow travel that I always dreamed of, listening to others stories while trying to make sense of my own.

In 2026, I came to my mother's hometown, Shanghai, to learn more Mandarin, something else I've always wanted to do.

I didn't expect this task to require its own set of emotional adjustments.

This time, though, I wasn't asked to reach or grasp, but to quit holding back parts of myself.

These days, when I want a break from looking at complicated Chinese characters and Chinese apps that move too quickly, I go to the park to watch elderly couples square dance.
